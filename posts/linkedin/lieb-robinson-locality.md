Most treatments of quantum many-body physics study one Hamiltonian at a time. Continuously varying parameters, disorder, and symmetry data get bolted on afterward, often informally. Part I of a six-paper research program on topological phases of matter asks whether the space of interactions itself can be given a rigorous, well-behaved structure from the start.

The approach comes from condensed mathematics, the framework Clausen and Scholze built to make topological data behave well under the same exact, derived operations that algebraists take for granted. We fix a Nachtergaele-Sims-Young locality function, show that the resulting space of symmetric interactions is a genuine Banach space, and then condense it: probe it with profinite sets instead of ordinary topological spaces.

The main result is that the Lieb-Robinson bound, the estimate that gives quantum lattice systems a finite information propagation speed, extends from a single system to an entire continuously varying family with a uniform velocity and uniform constants. That uniformity is the fact the rest of the series depends on: it is what lets a family of Hamiltonians be treated as a genuine morphism between condensed objects rather than a loose collection of separate calculations. A transverse-field Ising simulation in the paper checks the light cone and the velocity fit directly.

This is the first of five analytic building blocks in the series; the sixth paper shows how all five compose into a single modular research program.
