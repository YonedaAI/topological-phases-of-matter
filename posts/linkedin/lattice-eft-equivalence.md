Physical topological phases can be stacked: put two decoupled systems side by side and the result is a new phase in the same classification. That stacking operation is a genuine algebraic structure, and Part IV of our six-paper research program works out exactly what structure it is and what it buys us.

On the level of phases, stacking makes the set of phases a commutative monoid, with the trivial product state as its identity element and the short-range-entangled phases as exactly the invertible elements. Group-completing that monoid, in the same sense one completes the natural numbers to the integers, requires adding trivial ancillas (a "stabilization" move with a precise universal property), and applying the recognition principle for grouplike E-infinity spaces turns the invertible sector into a connective spectrum: the invertible condensed phase spectrum. Kubota's operator-algebraic Omega-spectrum construction already gives a rigorous carrier for its homotopy groups, and Aoki's solidification theorem supplies the analytic completion a condensed refinement needs.

We then set this microscopic, lattice-built spectrum against the Freed-Hopkins bordism classification from effective field theory, and state the comparison as a conjecture at three levels of strength (on phases, on spectra, and on families), rather than a proof. What is provable today: the one-dimensional case is complete via Ogata's index, and free-fermion phases match the tenfold-way K-theory table, with its Bott periodicities checked by machine.
