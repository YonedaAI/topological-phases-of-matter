Topological invariants of real materials, the integers that classify topological insulators and superconductors, are usually computed in operator K-theory, the K-theory of C*-algebras. Part II of our six-paper research program on topological phases of matter asks where that computation actually lives inside the condensed-mathematics framework the series builds on.

We treat the quasi-local observable algebra of a spin system as a condensed object and show its state space, the set of physically allowed expectation-value functionals, condenses to a compact Hausdorff object on which positivity is a closed condition. The result the rest of the paper leans on is Aoki's solidification theorem: for a real Banach algebra, solidifying its algebraic K-theory recovers the topological K-theory directly. That means the operator-K invariants used for free-fermion and disordered topological phases are values of an intrinsically condensed-mathematical functor, not just numbers computed by a separate, unrelated method.

We also give disorder a rigorous condensed home: Bellissard's crossed-product construction over a profinite disorder hull, with the condensation shown to be functorial in the hull's finite quotients. Five numbered conjectures mark what the paper does not settle, including a real/KKO refinement matching Kitaev's periodic table.
