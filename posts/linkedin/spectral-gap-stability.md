A spectral gap, the energy difference between a quantum system's ground state and its first excited state, is the central hypothesis behind almost every classification result for topological phases of matter. Part III of our six-paper research program confronts an uncomfortable fact about that hypothesis directly: Cubitt, Pérez-García, and Wolf proved in 2015 that whether a general translation-invariant lattice Hamiltonian is gapped is undecidable. No algorithm can settle it in general.

Given that wall, the paper does not attempt to prove existence of a gap or propose a decision procedure; both are ruled out by the undecidability result. Instead, it treats "gapped" as a hypothesis that defines a substack of the moduli space of Hamiltonians, and asks what can be proved about that substack's structure. We show the finite-volume gap is Lipschitz continuous in the interaction, so the gapped region is open at any finite resolution, and, on the frustration-free and locally-topologically-quantum-ordered stratum, we combine the Bravyi-Hastings-Michalakis and Michalakis-Zwolak stability theorems with Nachtergaele-Sims-Young's infinite-volume result to get a perturbation threshold that does not depend on system size.

The practical upshot: the set of gapped systems is provably stable and well-behaved exactly where the physics literature already needs it to be, without overreaching into territory the undecidability theorem closes off.
