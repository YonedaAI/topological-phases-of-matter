Classifying abstract topological phase classes is only half the problem. The other half, the one Part V of our six-paper research program takes on, is realizability: given an abstract class in a bordism or homotopy classification, does an explicit, physically sensible lattice Hamiltonian actually produce it?

We separate two different notions of realizability. The broad one asks for realization by any uniformly gapped local Hamiltonian. The narrow one asks for realization by a local commuting-projector model, the kind of exactly-solvable Hamiltonian most constructive results in the field actually build. On the positive side, every class in group cohomology is realized explicitly, by Chen-Gu-Liu-Wen-type commuting-projector models with the correct edge anomaly, and in one and two spatial dimensions, Ogata's operator-algebraic index is a complete invariant, so the realized set and the classified set coincide there.

On the obstruction side, the Kapustin-Fidkowski theorem is a hard no-go: no local commuting-projector Hamiltonian can carry a nonzero Hall conductance, so every chiral invertible class, the kind of phase a real Chern insulator or fractional quantum Hall state realizes, sits outside what commuting-projector models can ever produce. We do not route around this; we state it as a permanent constraint on any realizability claim, and record three open conjectures about what fills the gap, including the expectation that chiral phases are realizable by non-commuting gapped Hamiltonians even though they are provably unreachable by commuting projectors.
