🧩 Imagine two separate quantum systems, each in its own topological phase, placed side by side without letting them interact. The combined system is a new phase, and this "stacking" operation behaves a lot like addition.

Part IV of our series works out exactly what kind of mathematical structure stacking gives you. The phases with an inverse, ones you can cancel out by stacking with something else, turn out to form the interesting part. Completing that structure, in the same spirit as building negative numbers out of the counting numbers, produces a genuine higher-dimensional object called a spectrum.

Here is the payoff: physicists already have a separate classification of these phases from effective field theory, the Freed-Hopkins bordism classification. We compare the two, and state, as an explicit open conjecture rather than a proved fact, that the lattice-built spectrum and the field-theory classification agree. What we can already prove: in one spatial dimension the comparison is settled, and for free-fermion systems the match with the well-known "tenfold way" table holds, checked directly by computer.

Why it matters: this is an honest attempt to connect what you build on a lattice with what field theory predicts, without pretending the connection is already fully proved.
