⚛️ Here is a fact that surprises most people outside the field: there is a mathematical proof that no computer program can ever look at an arbitrary quantum lattice system and tell you, in general, whether it has an energy gap.

That is the Cubitt-Pérez-García-Wolf undecidability theorem from 2015, and it is a real wall, not a temporary gap in current technique.

Part III of our series takes that wall seriously instead of working around it. Rather than claiming we can decide whether a system is gapped, or that a gap always exists, we treat "gapped" as an assumption that defines the family of systems we study, and we ask a more honest question: on the systems where a gap does exist and satisfy certain physically standard conditions, is that gap stable if you perturb the system slightly?

The answer is yes, and we prove it with a real threshold that does not shrink as the system gets larger, using stability theorems from Bravyi, Hastings, Michalakis, and others. We also show the gapped region is well behaved (open, in the technical sense) at every finite resolution you can check by computer.

Why it matters: it draws an honest line between what mathematics can currently prove about topological phases and what remains out of reach, and it builds real structure inside that boundary instead of ignoring it.
