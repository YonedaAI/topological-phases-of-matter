🔬 Quantum particles on a lattice can only influence each other so fast, even though quantum mechanics has no built-in speed limit like light in a vacuum.

That effective speed limit is called a Lieb-Robinson bound, and it is the reason quantum information in these systems spreads out in a cone shape instead of everywhere at once.

The problem: physicists usually prove this bound for one system at a time. Real research needs to track how a system changes as you tune a knob, a magnetic field, a coupling strength, a disorder level. Does the light cone stay well behaved across the whole family, or could it blow up somewhere in the middle without anyone noticing?

Part I of our six-paper series proves it stays well behaved. We show the space of allowed interactions is a genuine mathematical vector space (a Banach space), turn it into a condensed object (a framework built for exactly this kind of continuously varying data), and prove the light-cone velocity stays uniform across the entire family, not just at each point checked separately. A simulation of a simple spin chain, the transverse-field Ising model, shows the cone directly.

Why it matters: this is the ground floor of a six-part research program building one mathematical home for topological phases of matter, disorder, and the transitions between them.
