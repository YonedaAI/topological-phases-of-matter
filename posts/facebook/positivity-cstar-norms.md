🧮 Physicists have numbers, literal integers, that tell you whether a material is a topological insulator. Those numbers come from a branch of math called operator K-theory.

The question Part II of our series asks: where does that machinery actually come from, and can it be built on the same rigorous foundation as the rest of the program?

The answer runs through a result by mathematician Ryomei Aoki. Take the algebra of observables in a quantum system, condense it (place it in the profinite-probe framework the whole series uses), and a process called solidification turns its algebraic K-theory into exactly the topological K-theory physicists already compute. Same numbers, but now they come from one consistent mathematical home instead of a separate toolkit bolted onto the physics.

We also handle disorder (impurities, randomness) properly, using a construction of Jean Bellissard that assigns a C*-algebra to a whole family of disordered systems at once.

Why it matters: the invariants used to classify real materials are not a special case sitting off to the side. They are one instance of a more general condensed-mathematical operation, one that also generalizes cleanly to disorder and to continuously varying families.
