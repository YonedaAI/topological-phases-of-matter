Here is the peer review for the manuscript "From Lattice Models to Effective Field Theories: Stabilization and the Invertible Condensed Phase Spectrum".

### Critical Issues

1. **Mathematical Error in Example 4.4 and Formal Verification (Sections 4 and 9)**
   * **Location:** Example 4.4 and Section 9 (Formal verification)
   * **Details:** The author claims that the Grothendieck group completion of $M = \langle a,t \mid t+a=t \rangle$ is $\Groth(M) = 0$. This is mathematically incorrect. While it is true that the element $a$ maps to the identity $0$ (since $a+t=t \implies [a]+[t]=[t] \implies [a]=0$), there is no relation forcing $[t]=0$. The group completion is therefore isomorphic to the free abelian group $\mathbb{Z}$ generated by $[t]$. For $\Groth(M)$ to be $0$, an additional relation such as $t+t=t$ would be required. Consequently, the claim in Section 9 that the accompanying Haskell code "confirms the collapse" to $0$ indicates that the formal verification code either contains a critical mathematical bug (e.g., miscomputing the Grothendieck group) or its output was severely misinterpreted. 

2. **Logical Contradiction in Stabilization and Group Completion (Remark 4.4)**
   * **Location:** Remark 4.4
   * **Details:** The author defines $\Phases_{d,G} = (\text{raw phases})^{\mathrm{st}}$ by declaring two systems equivalent if they match after adding *product state ancillas*. The text then incorrectly equates this with the *full* cancellative quotient $M^{\mathrm{st}}$ from Proposition 4.3. Quotienting by product states does *not* produce a fully cancellative monoid in the presence of intrinsic topological order (where $a+m = b+m$ for a non-trivial topological phase $m$ does not generally imply $a+e = b+e$ for a trivial product state $e$). Furthermore, the author claims that $\Phases_{d,G}$ "sits inside its own group completion" (which strictly requires the monoid to be fully cancellative, meaning group completion does not collapse any further distinctions) but immediately follows up by stating that group completion "can collapse distinctions" for non-invertible phases. These two statements are mutually exclusive and represent a deep conceptual flaw in how the algebraic stabilization of non-invertible phases is handled.

### Major Issues

1. **Ambiguity Regarding the Identity Element and Stabilization**
   * **Location:** Section 3 (Proposition 3.1) and Section 4
   * **Details:** In Proposition 3.1, the specific trivial state $\mathbf{1}$ (with 1-dimensional on-site spaces) is shown to be the identity of the raw stacking monoid. If physical stabilization merely means stacking with $\mathbf{1}$, it does nothing algebraically since $a \boxtimes \mathbf{1} \simeq a$. The text needs to clarify that in the un-stabilized equivalence class $\Weq$, product states of dimension $>1$ are *not* equivalent to $\mathbf{1}$ (because finite-depth circuits preserve local Hilbert space dimensions). This is precisely what makes stabilization by arbitrary product states a non-trivial algebraic operation. Without this clarification, the definition of stabilization appears mathematically redundant to a careful reader.

2. **Imprecise Proof of Proposition 4.3**
   * **Location:** Proof of Proposition 4.3
   * **Details:** In proving that $M^{\mathrm{st}}$ is cancellative, the author writes "because $[a]+[e]=[b]+[e]$ in $M^{\mathrm{st}}$ means $a+e+e' = b+e+e'$...". To prove full cancellativity, one must show that $[a]+[c]=[b]+[c]$ implies $[a]=[b]$ for an *arbitrary* element $[c] \in M^{\mathrm{st}}$, not just for the stabilization element $[e]$. While the algebra works out exactly the same way ($a+c+e = b+c+e \implies a \approx b$), reusing the symbol $e$ to represent a generic monoid element is confusing and renders the proof technically incomplete as written.

### Minor Issues

1. **Obsolete LaTeX Package**
   * **Location:** Preamble (`\usepackage{everypage}`)
   * **Details:** The `everypage` package has been obsolete since late 2020. LaTeX now has built-in shipout hooks. Using `\AddToHook{shipout/background}` should be preferred, as `everypage` will generate compilation warnings on modern TeX distributions and is incompatible with some modern document classes.

2. **Citation Formatting**
   * **Location:** Throughout (e.g., Section 1, `Part~I, \cite{paperLocality}`)
   * **Details:** Using a non-breaking space for citations (e.g., `Part~I~\cite{paperLocality}`) is recommended to prevent awkward line breaks where the citation bracket gets orphaned on the next line.

3. **Table Formatting**
   * **Location:** Table 1
   * **Details:** The use of `\hline` is functionally fine, but standard publication-quality typesetting for tables strongly encourages the `booktabs` package (`\toprule`, `\midrule`, `\bottomrule`) for better spacing and vertical presentation.
